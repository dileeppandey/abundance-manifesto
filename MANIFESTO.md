# The Abundance Manifesto
## A Framework for Moving from Artificial Scarcity to Regenerative Commons

---

## In Brief

We live in a world capable of providing for everyone — yet our economic and legal systems are designed as if scarcity is permanent. This manifesto argues that scarcity, in the 21st century, is largely a choice. A political and economic choice made to protect the profits of a few.

Drawing from the open-source software movement, distributed manufacturing, ecological economics, and cooperative governance, this document lays out five principles for a different path:

1. **Universal Access to Source** — knowledge, designs, and tools should be open to all
2. **Design Global, Manufacture Local** — share knowledge freely, make things locally
3. **Regenerative Circularity** — build systems that restore, not just sustain
4. **Cooperative Stewardship** — govern shared resources democratically
5. **Access Over Ownership** — abundance comes from sharing use, not hoarding stuff

This is both a philosophy and a practical guide. The technology exists. The philosophy is sound. What's needed is the will to act.

---

## Part I: Understanding Scarcity and Abundance

### 1.1 How Scarcity Became a Policy, Not a Fact

For centuries, economists assumed that resources are scarce and desires are infinite. In a world of genuine material shortage — not enough food, energy, shelter — competitive markets made sense as a way to allocate limited goods.

But something shifted. The 21st century introduced a new kind of scarcity: **artificial scarcity**. Digital goods can be copied infinitely at zero cost. Open-source tools have slashed the price of manufacturing. Renewable energy has no fuel cost. The old logic of scarcity no longer applies — yet the systems built around it persist.

Why? Because corporations and industries facing shrinking profits use intellectual property law, proprietary design, and platform control to enforce scarcity where none technically exists. It's not natural; it's manufactured.

Murray Bookchin saw this coming. In *Post-Scarcity Anarchism*, he argued that 20th-century technology had already created the conditions for meeting everyone's basic needs — the barriers weren't technical, they were political and social. Abundance isn't a fantasy. It's being blocked.

### 1.2 What Manifestos Do

The manifesto format exists for a reason: it galvanizes collective action around shared values.

The **Russell-Einstein Manifesto** of 1955, facing the threat of nuclear war, appealed not to nations or factions but to shared humanity: *"Remember your humanity, and forget the rest."* That's the spirit this document aims to carry.

The **Agile Manifesto** of 2001 offers a structural lesson. It didn't prescribe rigid rules — it articulated *values* emerging from real practice. It succeeded because it named what thoughtful people were already doing. This manifesto works the same way: it names the abundance practices already sprouting in open-source and maker communities, and gives them a coherent framework.

### 1.3 Why Economics Now Supports Abundance

Economist Jeremy Rifkin identified the key mechanism: **zero marginal cost**.

Marginal cost is what it costs to produce one more unit of something once your fixed costs are covered. For digital goods — software, music, designs, knowledge — this cost is essentially zero. Sharing an ebook costs the same whether one person downloads it or a million do.

Rifkin argues this logic is spilling into the physical world. Renewable energy has no fuel cost. Automated logistics is becoming cheaper. When you combine the communication internet with an internet of energy and physical production, the cost of delivering goods begins to drop dramatically.

This creates a "Collaborative Commons" — an economy that operates alongside the market, and increasingly competes with it. Incumbent industries, threatened by falling profits, fight back through rent-seeking: patents, proprietary lock-in, and platform monopolies that extract value without creating it. This manifesto is a defense of the emerging Commons against that enclosure.

## Part II: Principle 1 — Universal Access to Source

### 2.1 What "Source" Means Beyond Software

The free software movement established a simple but powerful idea: you only truly own a tool if you can see how it works, fix it, and improve it. This requires access to the source code.

The Abundance Manifesto extends this to the entire built world. "Source" means:

- **Hardware schematics** — the drawings needed to reproduce a machine
- **Bill of materials** — what parts are needed and where to get them
- **Fabrication files** — CAD/CAM files, 3D print files, circuit board designs
- **Documentation** — the guides and tutorials that translate data into human capability

The "Maker's Bill of Rights" puts it plainly: *"If you can't open it, you don't own it."* When a phone manufacturer uses glue instead of screws, or pentalobe screws no one else makes, they're not protecting anything — they're manufacturing dependency. Planned obsolescence is an economic strategy, not an engineering necessity.

### 2.2 Licenses: The Key to Keeping Knowledge Free

Not all open licenses are equal. This matters enormously for abundance.

| License Type | Examples | What It Allows | Risk for Abundance |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Permissive** | MIT, BSD | Anyone can use, modify, even close the source | Corporations can take open work and re-enclose it |
| **Weak Copyleft** | LGPL | Core stays open; can link to proprietary parts | Compromise — encourages participation, protects core |
| **Strong Copyleft** | GPL v3 | All derivatives must stay open | Ensures the commons grows — prevents re-enclosure |
| **Copyfair** | Peer Production License | Cooperatives use freely; corporations must pay | Funds the commons while protecting it from extraction |

The Abundance Manifesto favors **Strong Copyleft** and **Copyfair** models. A permissive license can let Amazon or Google take an open-source project, improve it in secret, and sell it back — the community gets nothing. Copyfair creates a fairer deal: share freely with those who share back, charge those who extract.

### 2.3 Transparency as Infrastructure

Robert David Steele, in *The Open-Source Everything Manifesto*, argued that secrecy isn't just ethically bad — it's inefficient. Classified government intelligence, proprietary corporate R&D, and paywalled academic research all create fragmented, "dumbed-down" understanding that leads to catastrophic failures.

In an abundance economy, openness is the mechanism of collective intelligence. When agricultural data, energy grid performance, and medical research are open, billions of people can work on problems simultaneously. That distributed problem-solving capacity is our greatest resource.

This means: publicly funded research should be public. Government-funded software should be open source. Publicly collected data should serve the public.

### 2.4 AI: The New Frontier of Openness

Artificial intelligence introduces a new, urgent dimension to the question of source access.

We are witnessing the collapse of the marginal cost of cognition itself. AI can now help design buildings, write code, solve equations, and generate scientific hypotheses. This is potentially the greatest driver of abundance in human history.

But it's also potentially the greatest enclosure in human history.

If AGI and its outputs — designs, software, scientific breakthroughs — are controlled by a handful of corporations, those corporations become the landlords of human intelligence. Every discovery gets sold back to us at a markup.

The Manifesto's position:
- **Intelligence is a commons.** Open-source AI models must be developed and maintained publicly.
- **If AI creates, it creates for everyone.** A cooperative that uses AI to crack fusion energy or design affordable housing has a moral obligation to release that breakthrough.
- **Hoarding knowledge now delays civilization.** In an era of accelerating AI capability, every day a breakthrough sits behind a paywall is a day of unnecessary suffering.

## Part III: Principle 2 — Design Global, Manufacture Local (DGML)

### 3.1 Cosmolocalism: Rethinking Where Things Come From

Current industrial globalization works like this: design happens at a corporate headquarters, manufacturing happens in low-wage factories far away, and goods are shipped around the planet. It's efficient at scale — but it's fragile, exploitative, and carbon-intensive.

DGML — or "Cosmolocalism" — inverts this logic:

> **Share designs globally (light bits travel for free). Make things locally (heavy atoms stay local).**

Instead of economies of scale (one giant factory), DGML creates economies of scope: a local FabLab with a 3D printer, CNC router, and laser cutter can produce a prosthetic limb, a tractor part, and a water filter using the same equipment and the same globally-shared open designs.

This democratizes the means of production. Economic gravity shifts from the global corporation back to the local community.

### 3.2 Case Study: Open Source Ecology

The most ambitious attempt to implement DGML is **Open Source Ecology (OSE)** and its **Global Village Construction Set (GVCS)** — a set of 50 open-source industrial machines needed to build a sustainable civilization: tractors, brick presses, circuit mills, hydrogen generators, and more.

The design philosophy is modular. A "Power Cube" — a standardized power unit — can drive both a tractor and a sawmill. Shared interchangeable parts dramatically reduce the cost and complexity of building and maintaining each machine.

**The honest lesson from OSE:** open source doesn't automatically mean industrial grade. The project faced real quality control challenges. Designs that look good on paper can fail in the field.

This is why the Abundance Manifesto borrows from the Agile Manifesto's principle: *"Continuous attention to technical excellence and good design enhances agility."* Open-source hardware must be held to rigorous engineering standards. The shift needed is from hobbyist culture to **civic engineering culture** — open, rigorous, and accountable.

### 3.3 Building the Infrastructure of Making

DGML requires physical nodes: community spaces where making happens.

Just as the 20th century built public libraries for books, the 21st century needs public **libraries for making** — FabLabs, makerspaces, and tool libraries serving as the nodes of a distributed production network.

This infrastructure builds **adaptive capacity**. When COVID-19 hit, centralized supply chains couldn't provide PPE fast enough. Distributed maker communities designed and locally manufactured face shields and ventilator parts within days. That resilience — the abundance of *capability* — is the real payoff.

| Feature | Mass Production | DGML |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Design | Proprietary, centralized | Open source, global commons |
| Production | Centralized factories | Local micro-factories & FabLabs |
| Logistics | Ship finished goods globally | Ship data globally; source materials locally |
| Resilience | Low (single point of failure) | High (distributed redundancy) |
| Environmental impact | High | Lower (local loops + repair culture) |

## Part IV: Principle 3 — Regenerative Circularity

### 4.1 Abundance Without Destruction

The most common objection to "abundance" is that the Earth is finite. And it's right — we've already crossed safe ecological limits in climate, biodiversity, and nitrogen cycles.

So how can we promise abundance for all without destroying the biosphere?

The answer is **Regenerative Circularity**: abundance of *utility*, not abundance of *extraction*.

Our current economy is linear: extract → process → consume → discard. This creates scarcity at both ends — depleting raw materials on one side, filling the world with pollution on the other.

A circular economy keeps materials in use at their highest value. A modular smartphone designed to last 10 years provides the same utility as five phones that last 2 years each — at 20% of the material cost.

### 4.2 Open Design Makes Circularity Possible

You cannot repair what you cannot understand. You cannot recycle a product if you don't know its chemical composition. Open-source design is the technical foundation of circular economy — it makes repair, remanufacturing, and repurposing possible.

Proprietary design is inherently wasteful. "Security through obscurity" blocks third-party repair, accelerating the scrapping of goods that still work. The **10R hierarchy** of circularity — Refuse, Rethink, Reduce, Reuse, Repair, Refurbish, Remanufacture, Repurpose, Recycle, Recover — requires open information at every step.

### 4.3 Beyond Sustainable to Regenerative

"Sustainable" means maintaining the current state. But we've already degraded the baseline — maintaining it isn't enough.

**Regenerative** means actively restoring: building soil, cleaning water, recovering biodiversity. The Global Village Construction Set includes machines for Compressed Earth Blocks — they use local soil, require minimal energy compared to concrete or fired brick, and are fully biodegradable. This is technology that works *with* planetary systems, not against them.

Abundance doesn't come despite ecological limits. It comes *through* efficiency and regeneration. This is how the Manifesto resolves the apparent contradiction between "more for everyone" and "a finite planet."

## Part V: Principle 4 — Cooperative Stewardship

### 5.1 The Commons Isn't Chaos — It's Democracy

Garrett Hardin's famous "Tragedy of the Commons" argued that shared resources are inevitably destroyed by self-interest — and became the intellectual justification for privatizing everything from water to genetic code.

Nobel laureate Elinor Ostrom spent her career proving Hardin wrong. Real communities *do* successfully manage shared resources — but only when they follow specific governance principles:

1. **Clear boundaries** — who the community is, what the resource is
2. **Participatory rule-making** — those affected by the rules help set them
3. **Monitoring and accountability** — there are consequences for abusing the commons

"Open" doesn't mean "unmanaged." It means **democratically managed**. These principles form the governance layer of the Abundance Manifesto.

### 5.2 Platform Cooperativism: Who Owns the Platform?

The "sharing economy" promised peer-to-peer liberation. What we got was Uber, Airbnb, and TaskRabbit — platforms that extract value from workers and users while externalizing costs like insurance and maintenance to those same workers.

This is rent-seeking with an app. It creates scarcity for the workers (low wages) and abundance for the shareholders.

The alternative is **Platform Cooperativism**: platforms owned and governed by the people who use them.

- **Stocksy** — a stock photography platform owned by the photographers
- **Fairbnb** — a vacation rental platform that reinvests profits into local communities instead of Silicon Valley

When the robots automate labor, the question "who owns the robots" becomes existential. If platforms are cooperatively owned, automation creates shared prosperity — shorter workweeks, higher incomes for members. If they're not, automation creates unemployment and concentration of wealth.

### 5.3 Essential Services as Commons

Water, energy, internet, transportation — these are not luxury goods. They're the foundation of a functioning life. Managing them through profit logic creates structural inequality.

The Manifesto advocates for **Public-Commons Partnerships (PCPs)**: the state enables communities to manage their own essential resources. Examples:

- Community-managed water systems
- Neighborhood energy cooperatives generating and sharing solar power
- City-owned broadband networks

This follows the principle of **subsidiarity**: decisions should be made at the most local level that makes sense. Centralization should be the exception, not the default.

| Feature | Corporate Platform | Platform Cooperative |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Ownership | Shareholders / VCs | Workers / Users |
| Goal | Maximize profit | Maximize member benefit |
| Data | Monetized, sold | Protected, used for service |
| Governance | Top-down | One member, one vote |
| Value distribution | Concentrated | Shared among members |

## Part VI: Principle 5 — Access Over Ownership

### 6.1 You Don't Need to Own Things to Use Them

Jeremy Rifkin identified a quiet shift in modern capitalism: the move from owning things to paying for access. In an era of rapid technological change, ownership is increasingly a liability. Assets depreciate, require maintenance, and become obsolete. What people want is the *utility* — the warmth, the mobility, the light — not the object itself.

The ownership model is also spectacularly wasteful. A private car sits idle 95% of its life. A drill is used for an average of 13 minutes total across its lifetime.

Shift to access: one shared car can replace 10–15 private cars. That's abundance — mobility for everyone — achieved with a fraction of the material footprint.

### 6.2 Product-as-a-Service: Incentivizing Quality

The access model changes what manufacturers are incentivized to build.

In an ownership economy with planned obsolescence: make it break, sell a new one.

In an access economy (Product-as-a-Service): the manufacturer retains ownership and sells performance. Rolls-Royce doesn't sell jet engines — they sell "power by the hour." Philips sells "light," not lightbulbs.

Under this model, manufacturers are incentivized to make products that are:
1. **Durable** — breakdowns cost them money
2. **Repairable** — easy fixing lowers service costs
3. **Modular** — upgrading is cheaper than replacing

This aligns perfectly with Principles 1 (open source) and 3 (circularity). The catch: access platforms must be cooperatively owned (Principle 4). Otherwise, we get digital feudalism — a handful of corporations owning everything, and everyone else renting their lives.

### 6.3 The Cultural Shift: From Hoarding to Contributing

Consumerism drives the accumulation of stuff as a proxy for security and status. The Abundance Manifesto argues for a cultural shift from **ownership** to **usership**.

When basic needs are reliably met, human motivation shifts. The research on post-scarcity societies consistently shows people pivot toward creativity, connection, and contribution. The "rat race" is a symptom of scarcity. In an abundance economy, status comes from what you contribute to the commons — open code, shared designs, art, knowledge — not from what you accumulate in private.

## Part VII: The Manifesto

*The following is the living document — the statement of values meant to motivate the movement.*

---

### THE ABUNDANCE MANIFESTO

**Preamble**

We are the heirs of a paradox. We live in a world of unprecedented technological capacity, yet we are bound by the economic logic of a bygone era. We have mapped the human genome, connected the globe in a neural network of light, and learned to weave matter from the atomic level up. Yet, we are told that we must compete for survival, that resources are scarce, and that the only path to prosperity is the enclosure of the commons.

We reject this manufactured reality. We declare that abundance is not a utopian fantasy, but a technical possibility and a moral imperative. It is the natural state of information, of renewable energy, and of human creativity. Scarcity, in the 21st century, is a policy choice — a choice to maintain the privileges of the few at the expense of the many.

We choose differently. We choose to build a world where the necessities of life are guaranteed, where the tools of creation are shared, and where the planet is respected as the source of all wealth.

---

**I. Radical Openness**
*(Universal Access to Source & Intelligence)*

We assert that the knowledge required to sustain life belongs to all. The source code of our tools, the blueprints of our homes, the genetic heritage of our food, and the data of our biosphere must be free to study, modify, and share.

In the age of Artificial Intelligence, we reject the digital feudalism where the few control the means of cognition. We pledge that the fruits of our machines — whether code, art, or scientific breakthrough — shall be returned to the commons. If our AI creates, it creates for humanity, not for the rent-seeking of the few.

We reject the black box. Transparency is the bedrock of trust. Openness is the engine of evolution.

---

**II. Distributed Materiality**
*(Design Global, Make Local)*

We shall design for the whole earth but build for our own neighborhoods. We reject the fragile, extractive chains of globalized mass production. Instead, we embrace the resilience of the local.

We will share knowledge globally at the speed of light, while manufacturing locally with the weight of atoms. By democratizing the means of production — from the micro-factory to the community garden — we reclaim our autonomy and reduce our footprint.

We are not just consumers. We are makers.

---

**III. Regenerative Circularity**
*(Abundance within Boundaries)*

We recognize that infinite growth on a finite planet is madness. True abundance is not a pile of discarded gadgets; it is a cycle of enduring utility.

We commit to designing systems that regenerate the earth — building soil, cleaning water, recovering materials. We pledge to refuse waste, to repair what is broken, and to build for generations, not quarters.

We flourish *within* the boundaries of our living planet, finding freedom in efficiency and stewardship.

---

**IV. Cooperative Stewardship**
*(The Democracy of Value)*

We reject the tragedy of unmanaged greed. The commons is not a free-for-all; it is a shared responsibility.

We advocate for the cooperative ownership of the platforms, infrastructures, and intelligences that we depend upon. Those who create the value — the workers, the users, the communities — must govern the value.

We build for stakeholders, not just shareholders. We replace the dictatorship of capital with the democracy of cooperation.

---

**V. Access Over Ownership**
*(The Freedom of Utility)*

We seek the lightness of access over the burden of possession. We recognize that the hoarding of assets creates scarcity, while the sharing of utility creates abundance.

We will build libraries of tools, fleets of shared mobility, and networks of shared energy. By maximizing the use of what we have, we liberate ourselves from the need for more.

We measure our wealth not by what we hold, but by what we can use together.

---

**Call to Action**

To the engineers: Design for repair.

To the artists: Create for the commons.

To the farmers: Restore the soil.

To the coders: Free the source and the AI.

To the citizens: Organize your communities.

The tools are in our hands. The blueprints are open. The future is waiting to be built. Let us leave the age of scarcity behind and step, together, into the age of abundance.

## Part VIII: How to Get There

### 8.1 The Copyfair Strategy: Funding the Transition

How do we pay for this within a capitalist economy?

The **Copyfair** license creates a membrane between the abundance economy and the scarcity economy:

- An open-source hardware cooperative releases a superior wind turbine design under a Peer Production License
- Other cooperatives use it for free → the commons grows
- A for-profit corporation can use it *only if they pay a license fee*
- That fee funds the cooperative's R&D — transferring wealth from the extractive sector to the generative sector

This solves the "Amazon problem": big corporations taking open-source projects, improving them in private, and giving nothing back. Copyfair makes free-riding economically untenable.

### 8.2 The Internet of Production

Just as HTTP standardized how information flows on the internet, we need standardized protocols for how physical production knowledge flows.

Two priorities:
- **Documentation standards** — enforcing formats like the Open Know-How Manifest to ensure "open" hardware is actually buildable by someone who wasn't in the room when it was designed
- **Distributed quality assurance** — cryptographic chains of custody for distributed manufacturing: if a FabLab prints a load-bearing structural part, there needs to be a verifiable record that it meets specs

### 8.3 The Partner State

The state's role needs to change from "protector of property" to "enabler of commons."

Concrete policy proposals:
- **Right to Repair legislation** — manufacturers must provide repair manuals and parts
- **Public Money, Public Code** — software and hardware built with tax money should be open source
- **Procurement preferences** — government contracts should favor cooperatives and circular businesses

### 8.4 The Jevons Paradox Problem

There's a real risk: as technology makes things more efficient and cheaper, total consumption often *increases* rather than decreases. Cheap energy might mean we just use far more of it.

This is why Principle 3 (Boundaries) and Principle 4 (Stewardship) aren't optional add-ons — they're load-bearing principles. Abundance must be governed by democratic limits: carbon caps managed as commons, cultural norms that measure wealth in time and relationships rather than accumulation. The goal is *time affluence* (working less, living more) not just *material affluence* (consuming more).

---

## Conclusion

The Abundance Manifesto is not a prediction. It is a project.

If we leave automation and AI to operate within the current scarcity-based market logic, the most likely outcome is extreme inequality and ecological collapse — a world of rent-seeking elites and a precarious majority.

By adopting the principles of Open Source, DGML, Circularity, Cooperativism, and Access, we steer toward a different future: not degrowth of misery, but post-scarcity of flourishing.

The technology is ready. The philosophy is sound. The only missing variable is the political will to execute. This manifesto is the spark for that will.
